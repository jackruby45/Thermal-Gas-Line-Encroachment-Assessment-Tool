Thermal–Gas Line Encroachment Assessment Tool

Rev-2.0, By Tim Bickford

About This Tool

The primary purpose of this tool is to evaluate heat transfer that will potentially impact existing or proposed gas lines in proximity to below-ground heat sources, such as steam, hot water, or superheated hot water lines.

How to Use This Tool

This guide provides detailed instructions for completing a comprehensive Thermal–Gas Encroachment Assessment. Following these steps will ensure accurate calculations and a well-documented report.

Before You Begin

  • Gather Information: Collect as much data as possible before starting. This includes as-built drawings, operating specifications for the heat source, gas line records, soil survey maps, and any previous field observations.
  • Understand Data Quality: Many fields allow you to specify if the data is "Actual," "Assumed," or "Unknown." Use these qualifiers accurately. They are critical for understanding the confidence level of the final results and will be reflected in the reports.

Step 1: Enter All Known Data

Navigate through the data entry tabs sequentially. The more complete and accurate the data, the more reliable the assessment will be.

  • Evaluation Information: Fill in the administrative details of the assessment. This includes the project name, location, date, and the names of all personnel involved. A clear project description is essential for context.
  • Heat Source Data: Document everything about the heat-producing line. This includes its type (steam, hot water), operator contact details, operating temperature and pressure, age, pipe material, dimensions, and insulation details.
  • Gas Line Data: Provide detailed information about the gas pipeline being assessed. This includes its operator, material (e.g., plastic, coated steel), dimensions, pressure, and its geometric relationship (orientation and distance) to the heat source.
  • Soil Data: Define the thermal environment. Select the native soil type, which auto-populates a standard thermal conductivity value that you can override if you have more specific data. Document moisture content and average surface temperature. Detail any special bedding material (like sand) used for either pipeline.
  • Field Visit: Record on-site findings. Document the date of the visit, personnel present, weather conditions, and crucial observations like visible signs of heat loss (melted snow, stressed vegetation), surface conditions, and proximity to other utilities.

Step 2: Perform the Calculation

Once all data has been entered, navigate to the Calculation tab. This tab serves two purposes:

  1. Review Your Inputs: First, it presents a complete, read-only summary of all the data you entered. Carefully review this summary to ensure everything is correct before proceeding.
  2. Run the Analysis: Click the "Calculate Temperature at Gas Line" button. The tool will perform the heat transfer calculations based on your inputs. The results will appear below the button, showing the calculated gas line temperature, the temperature rise above the ambient ground, and other key thermal metrics.

Important: The tool will display a prominent warning if the calculated temperature exceeds the material-specific allowable limits for the gas pipe or its coating. For plastic pipes, a conservative operational limit of 70°F is used for the primary warning to ensure long-term safety.

Step 3: Add Actionable Recommendations

Go to the Recommendations tab. Based on the calculation results and your overall assessment, add specific, actionable recommendations. You can add as many as needed. These could include recommendations for monitoring, mitigation (e.g., installing a heat shield), further investigation, or changes to operational procedures. These user-defined recommendations are a critical part of the final report.

Step 4: Generate Reports

The tool provides several reporting options to document and communicate your findings:

  • Final Report: (Admin access required) This is the most comprehensive output. It guides you through a series of contextual questions and then generates a formal, multi-section engineering report. This report synthesizes all input data, calculation results, risk analysis based on your answers, and your specific recommendations into a professional, readable format.
  • Latex Report: For users who need to incorporate the findings into formal scientific or engineering documents, this tab provides a complete summary report formatted in LaTeX code. You can copy this code and paste it into any LaTeX editor.
  • Assessment Form: This tab provides a clean, printable summary of all the data you entered. It is ideal for archiving, internal reviews, or as an appendix to other documents.

Step 5: Save and Load Your Work

Use the buttons at the bottom of the form to manage your assessment files.

  • Save Assessment: This downloads a single .json file containing all your entered data, questionnaire answers, and the generated final report. This allows you to archive your exact state of work.
  • Load Assessment: This allows you to select a previously saved .json file, which will repopulate the entire form and all its fields, enabling you to continue a previous assessment.

Heat Transfer Theory & Calculations

Core Principles

The calculations in this tool are based on a two-dimensional, steady-state heat transfer model. This approach makes a few key assumptions:

  • Steady-State: The system has reached thermal equilibrium. This means temperatures at any given point in the soil or on the pipes are no longer changing over time. This represents a continuous, long-term operational state, not a system that is just starting up.
  • Two-Dimensional: The model analyzes a 2D cross-section of the ground, assuming that heat transfer along the length of the pipe (the third dimension) is negligible compared to the radial heat transfer away from it. This is a valid assumption when the pipes are long and uniform.
  • Thermal Resistance Analogy: The primary calculation method uses the concept of thermal resistance, which is analogous to electrical resistance in a circuit (Ohm's Law, V=IR). In our case, Temperature Difference = Heat Flow × Thermal Resistance. Heat, like electricity, follows the path of least resistance.

Step 1: Calculating Total Heat Loss (Q) from the Source

The first step is to determine the rate of heat loss per unit length (Q) from the heat source pipe to the ground surface. This is governed by the total thermal resistance of all the materials the heat must travel through.

Q = (Ths - Tsurface) / Rtotal

The total resistance is the sum of the resistances of each component in the path: pipe wall, insulation (if any), and the soil itself.

Rtotal = Rpipe_wall + Rinsulation + Rsoil_hs

Step 2: Calculating Individual Thermal Resistances

Each component's resistance is calculated based on its material properties (thermal conductivity) and geometry.

Pipe Wall Resistance (Rpipe_wall)

This is the resistance to heat conduction radially outward through the solid wall of the heat source pipe.

Rpipe_wall = ln(Dhs_outer / Dhs_inner) / (2π * kpipe)

Insulation Resistance (Rinsulation)

This is the resistance through the insulation layer, which is typically much higher than the pipe wall's resistance. If no insulation is specified, this value is zero.

Rinsulation = ln(Dins_outer / Dhs_outer) / (2π * kins)

Soil Resistance from Heat Source to Surface (Rsoil_hs)

This calculates the thermal resistance of the soil between the outer surface of the heat source (or its insulation) and the ground surface above it.

Rsoil_hs = ln((2 * Zhs - Rsurface) / Rsurface) / (2π * ksoil)

The Method of Images Explained

The formula for soil resistance isn't as simple as for a pipe wall because the heat flows out in all directions, not just up. To solve this mathematically, a technique called the Method of Images is used. This method relies on a clever simplification:

  • The ground surface is assumed to be a perfect isothermal plane (it has a constant temperature, Tsurface, everywhere).
  • To create this condition mathematically, we pretend there is an imaginary "image" heat sink (a cold pipe) located above the ground at the same distance the real pipe is buried below it.
  • This imaginary sink perfectly cancels out the heat from the real source at the ground surface, creating the constant temperature plane we need. The formulas for soil resistance and temperature calculation are derived from this model.

Step 3: Calculating Temperature at the Gas Line (Tgas_line)

Once the total heat loss (Q) is known, we can calculate the resulting temperature at any point in the soil. The formula uses the same principles but is applied to the location of the gas line.

Perpendicular Orientation

For a perpendicular crossing, the calculation finds the temperature at the point where the two pipes' centerlines would intersect in a 2D plane.

Tgas_line = Tsurface + (Q / (2π * ksoil)) * ln((Zhs + Zgas) / |Zhs - Zgas|)

Parallel Orientation

For parallel lines, the temperature is calculated at the outer edge of the gas pipe that is closest to the heat source, representing the point of maximum thermal impact.

Tgas_line = Tsurface + (Q / (2π * ksoil)) * ln(dimage / dsource)

Where dsource is the straight-line distance from the gas pipe's edge to the center of the real heat source, and dimage is the distance to the center of the imaginary "image" source above ground.

  • dsource = sqrt( (C - Dgas_od/2)2 + (Zgas - Zhs)2 )
  • dimage = sqrt( (C - Dgas_od/2)2 + (Zgas + Zhs)2 )

Limitations of this Model

Important: This tool provides a valuable screening-level assessment based on established analytical models. However, it is essential to understand its limitations. For highly critical or complex scenarios, a more advanced numerical analysis (e.g., Finite Element Analysis) may be warranted.

  • Homogeneous Soil: The model assumes the soil has a single, uniform thermal conductivity (ksoil). It does not account for distinct layers of different soil types or localized variations.
  • No Moisture Migration: The model does not simulate the movement of water within the soil due to thermal gradients, which can significantly alter thermal conductivity over time.
  • No Transient Effects: The steady-state assumption means the model cannot predict how long it takes for the system to reach its final temperatures or how temperatures might fluctuate with a heat source that cycles on and off.
  • No Convection: The model only considers heat transfer by conduction. It does not account for heat transfer by groundwater movement (convection), which could dramatically increase heat transfer.

Role of Soil Bedding (Sand Padding)

This model uses a single value for the bulk soil's thermal conductivity (ksoil). The information collected about trench bedding (e.g., sand padding) is not used directly in a separate calculation layer but is crucial for informing the engineer's choice for the overall ksoil value.

For example, if the native soil is wet clay (high conductivity, ~0.90) but the heat source is known to be encased in a thick envelope of dry sand (low conductivity, ~0.20), the effective thermal resistance of the soil will be higher than if the pipe were in direct contact with the clay. An experienced engineer might select an effective ksoil value that is a weighted average, or more conservatively, use the native soil's value to assess a worst-case scenario where the bedding's insulating properties have been compromised over time.

Variable Definitions

  • Q: Heat loss per unit length (BTU/hr·ft)
  • Ths: Temperature of the heat source fluid (°F)
  • Tsurface: Temperature of the ground surface (°F)
  • R...: Thermal resistance (hr·ft·°F/BTU)
  • kpipe, kins, ksoil: Thermal conductivity of the material (BTU/hr·ft·°F)
  • Dhs_outer/inner: Outer/inner diameter of the heat source pipe (ft)
  • Dins_outer: Outer diameter of the insulation (ft)
  • Dgas_od: Outer diameter of the gas pipe (ft)
  • Zhs: Depth from ground surface to the centerline of the heat source pipe (ft)
  • Zgas: Depth from ground surface to the centerline of the gas pipe (ft)
  • C: Centerline-to-centerline horizontal distance between parallel pipes (ft)
  • Rsurface: Outer radius of the heat source pipe or its insulation (ft)
  • ln(): Natural logarithm
  • π: Pi (approx. 3.14159)

Example Calculation (Parallel Orientation)

Given Inputs:

  • Heat Source Temp (Ths): 350 °F
  • Ground Surface Temp (Tsurface): 50 °F
  • HS Pipe: 8" nominal diameter (OD = 8.625"), 0.322" wall, Carbon Steel (kpipe = 26)
  • Insulation: 2" thick, Calcium Silicate (kins = 0.034)
  • Soil: Moist Clay (ksoil = 0.70)
  • HS Depth (Zhs): 4 ft
  • Gas Pipe: 4" nominal diameter (OD = 4.5"), Depth (Zgas): 3 ft
  • Parallel Distance (C): 5 ft

1. Calculate Diameters (ft):

  • Dhs_outer = 8.625 / 12 = 0.7188 ft
  • Dhs_inner = (8.625 - 2*0.322) / 12 = 0.6651 ft
  • Dins_outer = (8.625 + 2*2) / 12 = 1.0521 ft
  • Rsurface = Dins_outer / 2 = 0.5260 ft

2. Calculate Resistances (hr·ft·°F/BTU):

  • Rpipe_wall = ln(0.7188 / 0.6651) / (2π * 26) = 0.00048
  • Rinsulation = ln(1.0521 / 0.7188) / (2π * 0.034) = 1.7853
  • Rsoil_hs = ln((2*4 - 0.5260) / 0.5260) / (2π * 0.70) = 0.6015
  • Rtotal = 0.00048 + 1.7853 + 0.6015 = 2.3873

3. Calculate Heat Loss (BTU/hr·ft):

  • Q = (350 - 50) / 2.3873 = 125.66

4. Calculate Temperature at Gas Line (°F):

  • Dgas_od = 4.5 / 12 = 0.375 ft
  • dsource = sqrt( (5 - 0.375/2)2 + (3 - 4)2 ) = 4.915 ft
  • dimage = sqrt( (5 - 0.375/2)2 + (3 + 4)2 ) = 8.508 ft
  • Tgas_line = 50 + (125.66 / (2π * 0.70)) * ln(8.508 / 4.915) = 65.8 °F
